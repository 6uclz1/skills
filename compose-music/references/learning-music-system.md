## Composition Ladder

Build music in this order:

1. Combine small patterns.
2. Change the combination over time.
3. Give each pattern a role.
4. Expand patterns into sections.
5. Arrange sections into a complete form.

This keeps composition concrete. Avoid starting with abstract mood words only; convert the mood into tempo, rhythmic density, scale color, register, and section energy.

## Beat System

Use a 16-step grid for one bar of 4/4:

```text
Steps:  01 02 03 04 05 06 07 08 09 10 11 12 13 14 15 16
Beats:  1e &a 2e &a 3e &a 4e &a
Times:  0.00 ...      1.00 ...      2.00 ...      3.00 ...
```

Core drum roles:

- Kick: pulse, weight, genre identity.
- Clap or snare: backbeat, accent, response to kick.
- Closed hat: subdivision and motion.
- Open hat: lift, release, phrase marking.

Start with one bar, then make a two-bar variation. Keep the first bar stable and let the second bar introduce a fill, missing hit, extra hat, or syncopated kick.

## Tempo and Genre

Use tempo as a compositional decision, not a playback setting:

- Dub: `60-90 BPM`
- Hip-hop: `60-100 BPM`
- House: `115-130 BPM`
- Techno or trance: `120-140 BPM`
- Dubstep: `135-145 BPM`
- Drum and bass: `160-180 BPM`

If the user specifies a genre, choose a tempo inside the typical range unless they ask for contrast.

## Pitch Palette

Choose the tonic first. Treat it as the home note for bass endings, melodic resolution, and final section stability.

Then choose a scale or mode:

- Major: bright, stable, open.
- Minor: darker, direct, familiar.
- Dorian: minor color with a lifted sixth, useful for electronic loops.
- Phrygian: tense, close, dark.
- Mixolydian: major color with a flat seventh, useful for grooves.
- Pentatonic: simple, strong, low-risk melody material.

Keep early sketches inside the palette. Add outside notes only when a tension target is named.

## Chords

Treat chords as simultaneous pitch groups that define harmonic color. Start with triads before extensions.

For each chord progression, specify:

- Roman numerals.
- Concrete chord names.
- Bars per chord.
- Register and voicing density.
- Where the tension increases and where it resolves.

Reordering a common progression is valid. The point is to alter the tension/release contour, not to prove novelty.

## Basslines

Basslines are low single-note patterns that usually reinforce chords while forming a rhythmic relationship with drums.

Design checks:

- Does the bass state the root clearly at phrase starts?
- Does it avoid masking the kick?
- Does it answer the kick, double the kick, or deliberately contrast it?
- Does it add a pickup or passing note only when the groove needs motion?

For dense electronic music, compose kick and bass as one low-end line before adding other low parts.

## Melodies

Melodies are single-note patterns, often higher than basslines, that define identity.

Write melodies by making a small motif, then changing one parameter at a time:

- Rhythm.
- Ending note.
- Register.
- Direction.
- Repetition count.
- Space between phrases.

Use a narrow range for hooks. Use range expansion for lifts, transitions, and peak sections.

## Song Structure

Think of a song as a block of time made from smaller pattern blocks. Use 4, 8, and 16 bar units by default.

Common electronic structure:

```text
Intro 8 | Groove 8 | Build 8 | Drop/Hook 16 | Break 8 | Drop Variation 16 | Outro 8
```

When the arrangement feels unclear, create the densest section first, then subtract parts to make the other sections.
